What makes you unreplaceable

Most people learn to use LLMs. A rare few can build, evaluate, and advance them. Here is what separates that tier.

Paper implementation habit
Implement every major paper from scratch — not using a library, raw PyTorch. Attention, RoPE, Flash Attention, LoRA. The person who has done this 20 times reads new papers in hours, not weeks.
Evaluation obsession
Build custom evals for every project. Most engineers skip this. The person who can define, measure, and prove model quality is worth 3 engineers who guess. Evals are your moat.
Mechanistic interpretability
Understand what's actually happening inside the model — circuits, attention heads, superposition. Fewer than 500 people in the world truly understand this. It's the hardest frontier and the highest moat.
Full-stack ownership
Pre-training → fine-tuning → inference → product → eval → iterate. Most ML engineers own one slice. You should own the entire loop. This is what senior researchers at labs do.
Write publicly
Blog every project. Write paper implementations on GitHub with clear READMEs. Tweet your evals. A recruiter finding your "I implemented Flash Attention from scratch" post is worth 10 resumes.
Domain × LLM intersection
Pick one domain (medicine, law, finance, code, science) and become the LLM expert in that domain. General LLM engineers are plentiful. "LLM + oncology" is nearly empty.
Safety & alignment depth
Red-teaming, constitutional AI, scalable oversight, interpretability-based safety. As LLMs enter high-stakes domains, safety engineers command the highest salaries. Almost nobody has this depth.
Systems engineering fluency
CUDA kernels, memory profiling, distributed training debugging, inference latency optimization. ML researchers who can also write systems code are extremely rare and paid accordingly.
The irreplaceable formula: Deep theory (phases 1–4) + implementation habit (papers) + a live product (capstones) + public writing + one domain niche. Someone who has all five is nearly impossible to replace — because they're not just "an LLM engineer." They're a system.
